Appendix: HyperPEER Part Two — Configurations, Numbers, and Reproduction Notes

Companion to "We Put Recursion on Trial, and the Control Group Won." Everything comes from the logs and metrics in https://github.com/MikeyBeez/HyperPEER (results directories named stage3_*). Hardware and software as in part one: a single RTX 5070 Ti, 16 GB, Pop!_OS 24.04, PyTorch 2.11 with CUDA 12.8. Weights and Biases runs in mikeybee/hyperpeer under stage3_*. Teacher, data, and the precise evaluation protocol are unchanged from part one: the fixed-k-256 PEER teacher on TinyStories, evaluated at 2.3227 nats over 200 fixed-seed validation sequences at context 512; the retrieval frontier references are k of 4 at 2.3673, k of 8 at 2.3387, and k of 16 at 2.3233.

B1. The recursion cell

Implemented as RecursiveGeneratedFFN in src/generator.py. Within each transformer layer's FFN slot: the state u starts as the block input; at each of T micro-steps the teacher's own RMSNorm is applied to u, expert matrices are produced, and the expert MLP output (GELU of the down-projections, summed against the up-projections) is added residually to u; the cell returns the total delta, which the block's standard residual adds once. T equals 2 everywhere below. Three modes for producing the micro-step's experts: rederive-step regenerates them at every micro-step from the current normalized state (function-space recursion, the hypothesis); rederive-once generates them once from the initial state and reuses them (activation-space recursion); the noise mode is rederive-once with Gaussian noise added to the state between micro-steps, standard deviation 0.02 times the state's root-mean-square, drawn with a fixed seed at evaluation time. With T equal to 1 the cell is numerically identical to part one's single-pass wrapper. The generator call is wrapped in non-reentrant activation checkpointing, as in part one.

B2. Round one — four warm-started arms at a gentle rate

All four arms warm-start from part one's final model (the stage-two checkpoint, 2.3573 on the precise protocol) and train 5,000 steps of plain next-token cross-entropy at learning rate 2e-5, warmup 100, cosine decay, batch 2, context 256, AdamW with betas 0.9 and 0.95, clip 0.5. Each arm logs an evaluation at step zero, before any training, to record the insertion penalty of bolting a second pass onto a one-pass model. Precise results: the T-equals-1 control 2.3509; same-experts-twice 2.3527; regenerate-each-step 2.3524; noise 2.3546. The spread is within evaluation noise. The criticism that prompted round two: 5,000 steps at a decaying 2e-5, on a near-converged model, with part of the budget spent recovering the insertion bump, cannot teach a new computational strategy — a null here is weak evidence.

B3. Round two — the hot retrofit and the attribution control

The regenerate-each-step arm was rerun for 10,000 steps at peak learning rate 6e-5 with warmup 500 (the rate chosen as a compromise: 1e-4 had detonated this generator during part one's distillation, and recursion adds feedback). Precise result: 2.3390 — at that moment the best model of the project, and on the small fixed-batch evaluation it scored fractionally better than the teacher on the same batches (2.2917 versus 2.2929), which the full protocol did not confirm. Because the hot run changed both the architecture and the schedule, the attribution control ran next: identical 10,000-step, 6e-5 schedule, T equal to 1. Criterion pre-registered before it finished: if the control lands near the recursive arm's number, the schedule gets the credit; only a control landing clearly worse (roughly 2.345 or above) would have left the win with recursion. The control scored 2.3372, slightly better than the recursive arm. Attribution: schedule. Recursion's marginal contribution: zero or slightly negative, at twice the per-token generator compute (about 0.75 steps per second versus 1.45 in training).

B4. Native recursion — the failed from-scratch distillation

A fresh-initialized generator with the T-equals-2 regenerate-each-step cell was trained by logit-KL distillation against the teacher, on a 20,000-step schedule at learning rate 3e-5 — the configuration that had distilled the single-pass generator without incident. It never began to converge: distillation KL flat around 0.7 (a zero-initialized student starts near 1.1; the single-pass run passed 0.04 by the same step count), student cross-entropy pinned in the 3.0 to 3.2 range — the no-expert-layer baseline — and drifting worse when it was killed at step 10,750. Conclusion drawn: with this loss and schedule, the one-pass solution is a prerequisite; recursion can be retrofitted onto a trained single-pass generator but not, apparently, learned from birth.

B5. The cumulative scoreboard

On the precise protocol, against the teacher's 2.3227, the lineage now reads: expert layers deleted, about 3.0; part one's small generator after 17 minutes of distillation, 2.4801; the scaled generator after full distillation, 2.3710; after stage-two next-token training, 2.3573; after the hot schedule (the part-two recipe, no recursion), 2.3372. The remaining gap is 0.0145 nats, about one and a half percent in perplexity (10.35 versus 10.20), down from 0.048 at the end of part one. The 2.3372 model is below the k-of-8 frontier point and above k-of-16. One protocol footnote: the hot recursive arm's precise evaluation ran as 100 batches of 2 sequences rather than 50 of 4 because the GPU was simultaneously training; same 200 sequences, same context, same seed protocol.

B6. Reproduction

From the repository root on a machine with the part-one setup: round one is python -m experiments.recursive_stage3 --arm X for X in t1_control, fixed_fn, rederive, act_noise, each defaulting to the stage-two checkpoint, 5,000 steps, 2e-5. The hot retrofit adds --steps 10000 --lr 6e-5 --warmup 500 --run-suffix _hot10k to the rederive arm, and the attribution control the same flags on t1_control. The native attempt is python -m experiments.distill_stage1 --steps 20000 --latent-n 16 --latent-d 512 --n-cross 2 --n-self 3 --lr 3e-5 --t-steps 2 --rederive step. Precise evaluations via experiments/eval_student.py with matching --t-steps, --rederive, and --noise-std flags — the evaluation must run the arm's own forward shape. Step-stamped checkpoints are written at 2,500-step intervals; the divergence playbook from part one (resume from the last good stamp at one-third the rate) was armed but never needed in part two.
